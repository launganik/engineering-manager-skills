# Team Health - Coaching Frameworks Reference

Loaded by: /team-health:prep, /team-health:summary
Purpose: Situational coaching frameworks that help managers ask better questions and have more productive 1:1 conversations. This document provides the substance behind inline coaching hints in prep output and growth context in summary output.

> These frameworks are tools, not scripts. Adapt them to the person and the moment. The goal is to help the manager have a better conversation — not to automate the conversation itself.

---

## How This Document Is Used

**In /team-health:prep:** After generating talking points in Section 4, each talking point receives an inline coaching hint — a 1-2 sentence tip drawn from the relevant framework below. The hint tells the manager *how* to raise the topic, not just *what* to raise.

**In /team-health:summary:** The Growth Context subsection in Section 2 references the framework most relevant to the person's stated career goals.

**Selection logic:** Match the framework to the situation using the mapping table in "Framework Selection" below. If multiple frameworks apply, use the one most relevant to the highest-priority talking point.

---

## Framework Selection

Use this table to select which framework applies to a given signal, commitment, or career context.

| Situation | Framework | When to use |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Signal flag (YELLOW or RED) on activity metrics (PRs, commits, tickets) | Curious Inquiry | Activity dropped — need to understand why without assuming the worst |
| Signal flag on review lag or response latency | Workload Check | Potential overload — need to explore capacity before adding pressure |
| Overdue commitment (>30 days open) | Accountability Reset | Manager made a promise and hasn't delivered — need to own it |
| Career goal: promotion or level-up | Growth Ladder | Person wants to advance — need to clarify what "ready" looks like |
| Career goal: new skill area or role change | Stretch Assignment | Person wants to grow in a new direction — need to find safe practice ground |
| Recurring concern entries (3+ in 6 weeks) | Pattern Naming | Same issue keeps appearing — need to surface the pattern explicitly |
| Win or positive signal trend | Strength Amplification | Something is going well — need to reinforce and build on it |
| New team member or sparse people log | Foundation Building | Relationship is new — need to establish trust and context |
| Meeting load >60% or calendar pressure | Boundary Coaching | Person is drowning in meetings — need to help them protect focus time |
| Confluence/doc activity high but code activity low | Contribution Reframe | Non-code contributions are significant — need to acknowledge and value them |

## Framework 1: Curious Inquiry

**When:** Activity signals have dropped but you don't know why. This is the default framework for YELLOW and RED signal flags on output metrics (PRs merged, commits, tickets closed).

**Core principle:** Lead with curiosity, not concern. The signal might mean nothing (vacation, deep work on a hard problem, context switching to help others). Your job is to learn, not to fix.

**Opening patterns:**
- "I noticed your PR activity looks different from your usual pattern the last couple of weeks. What's been going on?"
- "Your commit pattern shifted recently — are you working on something that doesn't show up in the usual metrics?"
- "I want to check in on your workload. How are things feeling day to day?"

**What NOT to say:**
- "Your numbers are down" — frames it as a performance problem before you know what's happening
- "I've been monitoring your GitHub activity" — feels like surveillance
- "Is everything okay?" — too vague, invites "yeah, fine" as a response

**Inline hint format for prep:**
> 💡 *Coaching: Lead with curiosity — "I noticed [specific metric] looks different from your usual pattern. What's been going on?" Avoid framing as a performance concern until you understand context.*

## Framework 2: Workload Check

**When:** Signals suggest capacity pressure — elevated review lag, high meeting load, increased response latency, or multiple stuck tickets.

**Core principle:** Assume good intent. High lag usually means too much on their plate, not that they don't care. Help them prioritize, don't add to the pile.

**Opening patterns:**
- "Looking at your week, it seems like you've had a lot of competing priorities. What's taking most of your attention right now?"
- "Your calendar looks pretty packed. Are you getting enough heads-down time for the work you need to do?"
- "I want to make sure I'm not overloading you. What could I take off your plate or re-sequence?"

**Follow-up moves:**
- Offer to reprioritize their work together (don't just tell them to "prioritize better")
- Ask what meetings they could skip or delegate
- Offer to run interference with other teams if cross-team requests are the issue

**Inline hint format for prep:**
> 💡 *Coaching: This looks like capacity pressure, not disengagement. Ask what's competing for their attention and offer to help reprioritize — don't just add another ask.*

## Framework 3: Accountability Reset

**When:** You have overdue commitments (>30 days open in the people log). This framework is for *you*, the manager — it's about owning what you said you'd do.

**Core principle:** Model the behavior you want to see. If you promised something and didn't deliver, acknowledge it directly. This builds trust faster than pretending it didn't happen.

**Opening patterns:**
- "I owe you an update on [commitment]. I said I'd [action] and I haven't followed through yet. Here's where it stands: [status]. I'll have it done by [new date]."
- "I want to circle back to something I committed to [N weeks ago] — [commitment]. I dropped the ball on that. Let me tell you what I'm going to do about it."

**What NOT to say:**
- Don't apologize without a concrete next step — "Sorry about that" without a plan feels empty
- Don't blame being busy — they're busy too

**Inline hint format for prep:**
> 💡 *Coaching: You have an overdue commitment here. Own it directly — state what you promised, acknowledge the delay, and give a concrete next step. This builds more trust than letting it slide.*

## Framework 4: Growth Ladder

**When:** The person's stated career goal involves promotion, leveling up, or taking on a leadership role.

**Core principle:** Make the implicit explicit. Most people don't know exactly what "ready for the next level" looks like. Your job is to translate organizational expectations into concrete, observable behaviors they can practice.

**Opening patterns:**
- "Let's talk about where you are relative to [next level]. I see you doing [X and Y] really well. The gap I'd want to close is [Z]. What do you think?"
- "For [target role], the key differentiator I see is [specific skill or behavior]. How are you feeling about that area?"
- "I want to make sure we're aligned on what [next level] looks like here. Can I share what I think the expectations are?"

**Follow-up moves:**
- Co-create a short list (3-5 items) of observable behaviors that demonstrate readiness
- Identify a specific project or situation where they can practice the gap area
- Set a check-in cadence for career progress (quarterly is typical)

**Inline hint format for prep:**
> 💡 *Coaching: Their goal is [goal]. Help make the path concrete — what specific behaviors would demonstrate readiness? Identify one gap to focus on and one project where they can practice it.*

---

## Framework 5: Stretch Assignment

**When:** The person wants to grow in a new skill area or explore a different role (e.g., IC exploring management, backend engineer wanting to learn frontend, engineer interested in product).

**Core principle:** Find safe practice ground. Growth happens at the edge of comfort, but too far outside it creates anxiety, not learning. Look for low-stakes opportunities to try the new skill.

**Opening patterns:**
- "You mentioned interest in [area]. I've been thinking about where you could get some practice with that. What about [specific opportunity]?"
- "If you wanted to try [new skill area], a safe way to start might be [suggestion]. How does that feel?"
- "I want to help you explore [area] without it being all-or-nothing. What's one small experiment we could try?"

**Inline hint format for prep:**
> 💡 *Coaching: They want to grow in [area]. Look for a low-stakes stretch opportunity — something they can try without it being career-defining. Ask what kind of support they'd need.*

## Framework 6: Pattern Naming

**When:** The same concern category appears 3+ times in the people log within 6 weeks. Something is recurring and needs to be addressed explicitly.

**Core principle:** Name the pattern without blame. Recurring issues often persist because nobody names them directly. Saying "I've noticed this coming up repeatedly" is different from "you keep doing this."

**Opening patterns:**
- "I've noticed [topic] has come up in our last few conversations. I want to make sure we're actually addressing it, not just noting it. What do you think is driving this?"
- "This is the third time [issue] has surfaced. I don't think it's a coincidence. Can we dig into what's going on underneath?"

**Inline hint format for prep:**
> 💡 *Coaching: This theme has recurred [N] times in [timeframe]. Name the pattern directly — "I've noticed this keeps coming up. What do you think is driving it?" — and commit to addressing the root cause together.*

---

## Framework 7: Strength Amplification

**When:** Signals show positive trends, or a recent win was logged. The person is doing well — use this to reinforce and build on it.

**Core principle:** Specific praise is 10x more impactful than generic praise. "Great job" is nice. "The way you structured the API review feedback made it easy for the team to act on — that's exactly the kind of technical leadership I want to see more of" is memorable.

**Opening patterns:**
- "I want to call out something specific you did well: [concrete example]. That's the kind of [behavior] that makes a real difference."
- "Your [metric/area] has been really strong lately. What's working well for you? I want to understand so we can build on it."

**Inline hint format for prep:**
> 💡 *Coaching: This is a win worth reinforcing. Be specific about what they did well and why it mattered. Ask what's working so they can replicate it intentionally.*

---

## Framework 8: Foundation Building

**When:** The person is new to the team, or their people log is sparse (fewer than 5 entries). The manager-IC relationship is still forming.

**Core principle:** Invest in understanding before advising. Early 1:1s should be heavily weighted toward listening. Learn how they work, what they care about, and what support looks like to them.

**Opening patterns:**
- "I want to make sure our 1:1s are useful for you. What's the most helpful thing a manager has done for you in the past?"
- "How do you prefer to receive feedback — in the moment, or in a 1:1? Written or verbal?"
- "What's something you wish your previous manager had known about how you work?"

**Inline hint format for prep:**
> 💡 *Coaching: This relationship is still forming. Prioritize listening over advising. Ask about their preferences, past experiences, and what good support looks like to them. Consider running /team-health:onboard [name] if you haven't already.*

---

## Framework 9: Boundary Coaching

**When:** Calendar signals show meeting load >60%, or the person has mentioned feeling overloaded. They need help protecting their time.

**Core principle:** You may be part of the problem. Before telling them to "push back on meetings," check whether *your* meeting invites are contributing to the overload. Then help them build the skill of saying no.

**Opening patterns:**
- "Your calendar this week was [X]% meetings. That's a lot. Which of those meetings are actually valuable for you?"
- "I want to help you protect more focus time. Are there meetings you're attending out of obligation that we could drop or make optional?"
- "Let me look at what I'm pulling you into — am I contributing to the calendar pressure?"

**Inline hint format for prep:**
> 💡 *Coaching: Meeting load is high. Before suggesting they decline meetings, check if you're part of the problem. Then help them identify which meetings they could skip, delegate, or make async.*

---

## Framework 10: Contribution Reframe

**When:** Confluence/doc activity is high but code-level signals (PRs, commits) are lower than baseline. The person may be doing valuable non-code work that isn't visible in the standard metrics.

**Core principle:** Not all impact shows up in git log. Design docs, RFCs, architecture reviews, onboarding guides, and cross-team coordination are high-leverage work that often goes unrecognized. Make sure your 1:1 acknowledges this.

**Opening patterns:**
- "I see you've been active on [N Confluence pages] recently. That's important work — tell me about what you've been writing."
- "Your code output looks lower this sprint, but I'm guessing that's because you've been focused on [docs/design/coordination]. Is that right?"
- "I want to make sure we're valuing your documentation and design work, not just counting PRs. How should we make this visible to the team?"

**Inline hint format for prep:**
> 💡 *Coaching: Code metrics are lower but doc/design activity is up. This is likely high-leverage work — acknowledge it explicitly and discuss how to make it visible. Don't frame lower PRs as a concern.*

## Applying Coaching Hints in Prep Output

When generating talking points in /team-health:prep Section 4 ("Suggested Talking Points"):

1. After generating each talking point question, determine which framework applies using the Framework Selection table above.
2. Append the inline hint from that framework immediately below the talking point.
3. Customize the hint: replace placeholders like [specific metric], [goal], [area], [N], [timeframe] with actual data from the person's signals and people log.
4. Maximum one hint per talking point. If multiple frameworks could apply, choose the one most relevant to the specific data driving that point.
5. If no framework clearly applies (e.g., a general check-in point), omit the hint — don't force it.

**Example output in prep:**

```
1. Your PR review count dropped from an average of 4.3/week to 1 this week. What's been competing for your review time? *(source: signal — GitHub PR review count below baseline)*
   💡 *Coaching: Lead with curiosity — "I noticed your review pattern looks different recently. What's been going on?" Avoid framing as a performance concern until you understand context.*

2. You committed to connecting Derese with the platform team lead 35 days ago. Where does that stand? *(source: commitment — overdue >30 days)*
   💡 *Coaching: You have an overdue commitment here. Own it directly — state what you promised, acknowledge the delay, and give a concrete next step.*

3. Derese mentioned interest in moving toward a tech lead role. What specific behaviors would signal readiness for that? *(source: career — stated goal)*
   💡 *Coaching: Help make the path concrete — what observable behaviors demonstrate readiness? Identify one gap to focus on and one project where they can practice it.*
```

## Applying Growth Context in Summary Output

When generating Section 2 ("Career Context") of /team-health:summary:

1. Read the person's `career_context.stated_goals` from their people log.
2. For each goal, identify the most relevant framework from the Framework Selection table.
3. Output 1-2 sentences in the "Growth context" subsection:
   - "Based on their goal of [X], the **[Framework Name]** approach applies — [one-line summary of the framework's core principle]."
4. If the person has career_context.notes with strengths or growth areas from onboarding, reference those:
   - "Noted strengths: [from notes]. Growth areas: [from notes]. The [Framework Name] framework can help bridge from current strengths to stated goals."
5. If no goals are recorded, suggest onboarding:
   - "No career goals recorded — consider running /team-health:onboard [name] to capture growth context."
